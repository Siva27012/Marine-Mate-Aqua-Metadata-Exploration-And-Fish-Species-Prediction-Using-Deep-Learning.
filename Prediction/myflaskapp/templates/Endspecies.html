Ganges Shark

Glyphis gangeticus

The Ganges shark (Glyphis gangeticus) is a critically endangered species found in the Ganges River and the Brahmaputra River in India and Bangladesh. It is the largest living member of the family Labridae and is uniquely adapted to freshwater habitats. Despite its rarity, it faces significant threats due to habitat degradation, pollution, and overfishing. Conservation efforts are crucial to protect this remarkable aquatic species.

Humphead Wrasse

Cheilinus undulates

The humphead wrasse (Cheilinus undulatus), also known as the Napoleon wrasse or Māori wrasse, is a remarkable marine fish found primarily on coral reefs in the Indo-Pacific region. It is the largest living member of the family Labridae and can reach lengths of up to 2 meters. Its distinctive features include thick lips, two black lines behind its eyes, and a prominent hump on the forehead of larger adults. Humphead wrasses are protogynous hermaphrodites, transitioning to males around 9 years old. They feed on invertebrates and play a vital role in reef ecosystems.

Pondicherry shark

Carcharhinus hemiodon

The Pondicherry shark (Carcharhinus hemiodon) is an extremely rare species of requiem shark, belonging to the family Carcharhinidae. This small and stocky gray shark grows not much longer than 1 meter (3.3 feet) and has a fairly long, pointed snout. Despite its scarcity, it plays a vital role in maintaining the balance of marine ecosystems. Conservation efforts are crucial to protect this unique and enigmatic shark.

Dusky grouper

Epinephelus marginatus

The dusky grouper (Epinephelus marginatus) is a sturdy species of predatory fish with a large head, round eyes, and a protruding jaw. It is also known as the yellowbelly grouper or yellowbelly rock cod. Its coloration ranges from dark reddish brown to greyish, often with yellowish gold countershading on the ventral surfaces. Despite its robust appearance, this grouper plays a vital role in maintaining the balance of marine ecosystems, particularly in Mediterranean and Atlantic waters.

Southern meagre

Argyrosomus japonicus

The Southern meagre (Argyrosomus hololepidotus), also known as the Madagascar meagre or southern meagre, is a fascinating marine fish species belonging to the family Sciaenidae, which includes drums and croakers. It is endemic to Madagascar and the Indian Ocean. First described in 1801, this fish has a unique name that possibly reflects its origin. The Southern meagre’s habitat includes coastal waters and estuaries, where it plays an essential role in the marine ecosystem.

Green turtle

Chelonia mydas

The green sea turtle (Chelonia mydas), also known as the green turtle, black (sea) turtle, or Pacific green turtle, is a species of large sea turtle belonging to the family Cheloniidae. It is the only herbivore among different turtle species. Contrary to its name, the green turtle’s shell color is not green; it is named after the greenish color of its cartilage and fat. In the Eastern Pacific, a subgroup of green turtles with darker shells is locally referred to as black turtles. These ancient mariners play a crucial role in maintaining the health of our oceans and seagrass habitats. Let’s continue to protect and appreciate these remarkable sea turtles!

Irrawaddy dolphin

Orcaella brevirostris

The Irrawaddy dolphin (Orcaella brevirostris) is a fascinating euryhaline species of oceanic dolphin found in scattered subpopulations near sea coasts and in estuaries and rivers in parts of the Bay of Bengal and Southeast Asia. It closely resembles the Australian snubfin dolphin (of the same genus, Orcaella), which was not described as a separate species until 2005. Its color ranges from slate blue to slate gray. Although found in much of the riverine and marine zones of South and Southeast Asia, the only concentrated lagoon populations are found in Chilika Lake in Odisha, India, and Songkhla Lake in southern Thailand.
