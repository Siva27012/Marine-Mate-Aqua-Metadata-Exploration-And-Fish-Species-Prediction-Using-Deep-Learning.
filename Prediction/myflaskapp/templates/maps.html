Kattupalli

Kattupalli Port, situated in North Chennai, is a state-of-the-art multi-cargo port offering seamless cargo movement with 24x7 congestion-free access roads. It boasts dedicated facilities for Direct Port Delivery clearance, on-dock Container Freight Station, and flexible carting services. With deep draft berths and multipurpose terminals, it efficiently handles diverse cargo including Fertilizer, Agri, Mines & Minerals, Steel & Project Cargo. Acquired by Adani ports and special economic zone (APSEZ) in 2018, the port's infrastructure supports simultaneous handling of multiple vessels and ensures swift vehicle turnaround. Strategically located in Thiruvallur district, it serves the hinterland of North Tamilnadu, Chennai Region, Bangalore region, and South Andhra Pradesh, facilitating trade with a capacity of 1.2 million TEUs per annum.

Ennore Minor Port

Ennore Port, also known as Kamarajar Port Limited, stands as a significant minor port in Tamil Nadu, India. Equipped with modern infrastructure and state-of-the-art facilities, Ennore Port efficiently handles various types of cargo, including bulk, breakbulk, and containerized cargo. Its strategic location and excellent connectivity via road and rail networks further enhance its importance as a maritime hub. Committed to environmental sustainability, the port implements eco-friendly practices and initiatives for pollution control and waste management. Contributing to the economic development of Tamil Nadu, Ennore Port attracts investments, generates employment, and drives international trade. With ongoing expansion plans and development initiatives, it aims to strengthen its position as a leading maritime gateway in South India.

Thirukkuvalai

The Thirukkuvalai Port is a deep-water multi-modal port being developed as a part of an integrated port and power project in Nagapattinam, Tamil Nadu. It is being developed by Tridem Port & Power Company Pvt. Ltd.[1] The entire project will entail an investment of about INR 100 billion with 45 billion in Phase I which includes 15 billion for the port development.[2] The Port is designed to handle 40 Mtpa. This project was initiated during the tenure of the DMK party in the state of Tamil Nadu.[3] The name of the project in its geographical location is misleading, named after the non-littoral locality of Thirukkuvalai (in the taluka of the same name), which is the birthplace of M. Karunanidhi, the major leader of the DMK party. The port project is in fact planned in the locality of Vettaikkaraniruppu, located in the Kilvelur taluka, between Velankanni and Vedaranyam. This locality is also closer to Velankanni and is easier to access from the latter as well as from the town of Vedaranyam.

Udangudi

The Udangudi Port project in Tamil Nadu, spearheaded by the Tamil Nadu Generation and Distribution Corporation (TANGEDCO), represents a significant endeavor aimed at facilitating the importation of coal for its ambitious 1600 MW Udangudi supercritical thermal power project in the Thoothukudi District. Central to this initiative is the construction of an open sea jetty intended to serve as a pivotal point for receiving coal shipments critical to powering the thermal plant. The decision to develop this captive port underscores TANGEDCO's strategic vision to ensure a reliable and efficient supply chain for the Udangudi power project. By establishing a dedicated marine facility, TANGEDCO seeks to streamline the logistics involved in importing coal, a key component for the operation of the supercritical thermal power plant. The port's location in Udangudi holds geographical significance, ideally positioned to facilitate the transportation of coal via maritime routes, optimizing accessibility and minimizing logistical challenges. Furthermore, the development of Udangudi Port aligns with broader regional economic objectives, potentially fostering job creation and stimulating local commerce through associated industries and services. This initiative not only addresses TANGEDCO's immediate operational needs but also signals a long-term commitment to infrastructure development in the region, with potential ripple effects on the local economy and broader energy landscape.

Area 1

India's western sea border encompasses its maritime boundaries along the Arabian Sea, part of the northern Indian Ocean. These boundaries extend up to 200 nautical miles (370.4 km) from the coastline, defining India's Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) and granting sovereign rights over marine resources within this zone. India's maritime borders in the Arabian Sea are critical for trade, security, and strategic interests. The Arabian Sea connects India with the Arabian Peninsula and the Horn of Africa, serving as a key route for maritime trade. Major ports along India's western coastline, including Kandla, Mumbai, Mangalore, and Kochi, facilitate the import and export of goods ranging from crude oil and petroleum products to minerals, agricultural produce, and manufactured goods. These ports are vital economic gateways, supporting India's external trade and economic growth.

India East

India's eastern sea border encompasses its maritime boundaries in the Bay of Bengal and the Indian Ocean. These boundaries, extending up to 200 nautical miles (370.4 km) from the coastline, delineate India's Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ), granting sovereign rights over marine resources. Maritime disputes with Bangladesh and Myanmar over the Bay of Bengal's boundaries prompt diplomatic negotiations. India shares maritime borders with countries like Bangladesh, Myanmar, Thailand, Indonesia, and Malaysia, delineated through international treaties. Given the region's strategic significance, India maintains a naval presence to safeguard interests and ensure security. Collaborative efforts with neighboring nations bolster maritime security, combating piracy, smuggling, and other illicit activities. India's engagement underscores the importance of protecting its maritime interests in the dynamic eastern sea border region.

India Andaman

India's maritime border in the Andaman Sea is delineated by its Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ), extending up to 200 nautical miles (370.4 km) from its coastline. Situated to the east of the Andaman and Nicobar Islands, this expansive maritime zone confers upon India sovereign rights over the exploration and utilization of marine resources within its bounds, encompassing fishing, oil and gas exploration, and other economic activities. Adjacent to neighboring countries like Myanmar, Thailand, and Indonesia, India's maritime boundaries in the Andaman Sea adhere to international maritime laws and agreements. Given its strategic position along crucial shipping routes and its proximity to significant maritime chokepoints such as the Strait of Malacca, the Andaman Sea holds immense importance for India's maritime security framework. The presence of Indian naval bases strategically located in the Andaman and Nicobar Islands reinforces India's surveillance capabilities and facilitates the monitoring of maritime activities in the region. Furthermore, India actively engages in regional cooperation initiatives with neighboring nations to address common challenges such as piracy, illegal fishing, and maritime terrorism, thereby promoting stability and security in the Andaman Sea region.

Srilanka Seaborder

Sri Lanka's maritime border, encompassing its territorial waters, exclusive economic zone (EEZ), and continental shelf, is a crucial aspect of the island nation's sovereignty and strategic significance in the Indian Ocean region. Within its territorial waters extending 12 nautical miles from its coastline, Sri Lanka exercises full control over shipping, fishing, and other activities. Beyond this zone, Sri Lanka's EEZ stretches up to 200 nautical miles, granting exclusive rights to explore, exploit, and manage marine resources. Additionally, Sri Lanka maintains sovereignty over its continental shelf, further extending its maritime domain. Maritime boundaries with neighboring countries like India and the Maldives are defined through bilateral agreements and international conventions. Situated along major international shipping routes, Sri Lanka's maritime borders are strategically important, with ports like Colombo serving as vital transshipment hubs. Ensuring maritime security is paramount, and the Sri Lankan Navy plays a key role in patrolling and enforcing maritime law to combat piracy, illegal fishing, smuggling, and other maritime threats. In essence, Sri Lanka's maritime border not only contributes significantly to its economic development but also underscores its role as a pivotal player in regional maritime affairs.
